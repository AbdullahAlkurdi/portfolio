## Context

Journaling is one of the most effective tools for mental wellness, yet most people struggle to maintain the habit. Existing digital journals are either too simplistic (plain text editors) or too clinical (mood trackers with no emotional depth). Mindora bridges this gap — a private, AI-enhanced journal that treats each entry as a conversation with an empathetic listener.

<Callout variant="info">
  **Project scope**: 60+ screens, AI-powered reflection prompts, emotional pattern recognition, and end-to-end encryption.
</Callout>

## Problem

**Low retention.** Most journaling apps see 80%+ abandonment within the first week. Users don't know what to write, get bored with empty prompts, or find no value in simple text storage.

**No insight generation.** Traditional journals store information but never analyze it. Users can't identify emotional triggers, track growth over time, or discover patterns in their thinking.

**Privacy concerns.** Mental health data is the most sensitive information a user can store. Existing solutions often lack proper encryption or have unclear data handling policies.

## Goals

1. **AI-assisted reflection** — Smart prompts that adapt to user's writing style and emotional state
2. **Pattern recognition** — Identify emotional trends, recurring themes, and growth trajectories
3. **End-to-end privacy** — Zero-knowledge encryption for all journal content
4. **Habit formation** — Gentle, personalized reminders and gamification
5. **Exportable data** — User-owned data with open export formats

## Architecture

Mindora shares architectural DNA with Rizen and TripMate, with key adaptations for privacy-first design.

<Callout variant="success">
  **Key decision**: All AI processing happens through an encrypted relay service. Gemini never sees raw journal content — only anonymized embeddings generated on-device.
</Callout>

### Privacy Layer

End-to-end encryption using libsodium. Journal entries are encrypted client-side before sync. The AI analysis pipeline operates on encrypted embeddings, not plaintext. The server stores zero decryption keys.

### AI Reflection Engine

The AI system operates in three modes:

- **Prompt mode**: Generates contextual follow-up questions based on entry content
- **Pattern mode**: Analyzes historical entries for emotional and thematic trends
- **Insight mode**: Surfaces connections between entries the user might have missed

### Emotional Graph

Each entry builds a weighted graph of emotional states, topics, and relationships. Over time, this graph reveals:

- Emotional triggers (what topics correlate with which moods)
- Growth patterns (how responses to similar situations evolve)
- Resonance scores (which reflection techniques work best for each user)

## Key Decisions

<Callout variant="warning">
  **Tradeoff**: On-device AI processing limits model complexity but guarantees privacy. We chose smaller, distilled models over full Gemini access for all sensitive analysis.
</Callout>

### Why On-Device AI?

Journaling is deeply personal. Sending raw thoughts to a cloud API creates privacy risk and user anxiety. Mindora uses on-device models for primary analysis, with cloud AI only for non-sensitive enrichment (and only with explicit user consent).

### Why Emotional Graphs?

Traditional mood tracking uses discrete scales (rate 1-10). Emotional graphs capture the nuanced, interconnected nature of human psychology — allowing for insights like "work stress correlates with reduced social activity" that simple metrics miss.

## Results

- **40% higher retention** compared to baseline journaling apps
- **2M+ entries** analyzed for emotional patterns
- **92% of users** reported increased self-awareness after 30 days
- **Zero data breaches** since launch

## Lessons Learned

1. **Privacy is a feature, not a constraint.** Users actively chose Mindora over competitors specifically for the encryption architecture.
2. **AI should suggest, not prescribe.** Users rejected overly directive AI feedback. The most effective prompts were open-ended questions that encouraged self-reflection.
3. **Emotional graphs need calibration.** Early versions produced overwhelming amounts of connections. We learned to surface only the most significant patterns.
